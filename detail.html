In 1894, Alphonse Mucha designed a last-minute poster for the play Gismonda, and it became a sensation. Bernhardt, happy with how the artist captured her stature, signed a 6-year contract with Mucha, effectively jumpstarting his career.

Alphonse Mucha, Gismonda, 1894, color lithograph, 85 x 29 3/16 in., Mucha Foundation.

La Dame aux Camelias (The Lady of the Camellias) was Bernhardt's most popular show in America. In 1906, during her third stop in Kansas City, a vote was held to determine her one-night-only performance, and this play won out.

Alphonse Mucha, La Dame aux Camelias, 1896, color lithograph, 82 5/16 x 29 1/2 in., Mucha Foundation.

Bernhardt (left) playing the part of Hamlet (1899)

Though Bernhardt began acting in female roles, she gained fame for playing masculine parts called "trouser roles" that showed her physicality and dramatic power.

"Le duel d'Hamlet," directed by Clément-Maurice Gratioulet, performances by Sarah Bernhardt, Pierre Magnier, and Suzanne Seylor, Soc. Phono-Cinéma-Théâtre, 1900.

James Lafayette, Irish, 1853–1923, Sarah Bernhardt as Hamlet, about 1885–1900, photochemical print, Library of Congress.

Bernhardt crafted herself as a sphinx to represent her ability to transform on and off stage. When touring abroad, she sometimes exhibited a cast of this self-portrait outside the performance hall.

Sarah Bernhardt, Fantastic Inkwell (Self-Portrait as a Sphinx), 1880, bronze, 12 1/2 x 13 3/4 x 12 1/2 in., Museum of Fine Arts, Boston.

Bernhardt was friends with many artists, including painter Louise Abbéma (1853–1927). Their friendship deepened into a romantic relationship, and they often depicted each other in their work.

Sarah Bernhardt, Bust of Louise Abbéma, 1878, marble, 16 1/2 x 10 5/8 x 8 11/16 in., Musée d'Orsay, Paris.

Although famous for her acting, Bernhardt was passionate about all forms of art. She would often sculpt, paint, design dresses, and even direct plays.

Sarah Bernhardt, Le retour de l'église (Return from Church), 1879, oil on board, 15 x 9 in., Royal Holloway, University of London.

Bernhardt sculpted this piece after hearing the tragic story of the model, who had recently lost her son. Allegedly, Bernhardt took anatomy classes to better capture the intensity of the story and to accurately honor the model's grief.

Sarah Bernhardt, Après la tempête (After the Storm), 1876, marble, 29 1/2 x 24 x 23 in., National Museum of Women in the Arts.

In the late 1800s, there was an interest in morbid, decadent, and gothic sensibilities. Bernhardt was a fan of the macabre. Some speculate her interest stemmed from her fragile health as a child or from a desire to ward off the fear of dying.

Sarah Bernhardt, A Girl and Death, 1879, lithograph, 6 1/8 x 5 3/16 in., Wellcome Collection, London.

"Now, Then, Camille."
Kansas City Times
May 11, 1887

American fans often referred to Bernhardt as "Camille," derived from the title of her famous play La Dame aux Camelias (The Lady of the Camellias). During Bernhardt's first show in Kansas City, the Gillis Opera House's curtain mistakenly hit a table due to a stagehand's error. Allegedly, Bernhardt chased the stagehand and would not perform until he left.

"Now, Then, Camille.," Kansas City Times, May 11, 1887, p. 10.

In 1906, during Bernhardt's third stop in Kansas City, she performed La Dame aux Camelias (The Lady of the Camellias) at Convention Hall. It broke all records as the largest known indoor audience for dramatic theater anywhere in the world.

Reproduced in Felicia Hardison Londré, The Enchanted Years of the Stage: Kansas City at the Crossroads of American Theater, 1870–1930, University of Missouri, 2007.
